Biodiversity Conservation

Managing woodland for biodiversity in the UK requires an understanding of traditional practices, such as coppicing, and the consequences of long-term non-management.

Many woodland species evolved in landscapes where trees were regularly cut or grazed, creating open, sunlit conditions alongside denser areas.

Where management has ceased, canopies often close, increasing shade and reducing the diversity of ground flora, shrubs, and insects that depend on light and structural variation.

This loss of habitat complexity is particularly damaging in the UK’s fragmented woodland landscape, where species may be unable to recolonise once lost.

Reintroducing management such as coppicing can restore light to the woodland floor, encourage flowering plants, and improve habitat quality, especially when combined with efforts to reconnect isolated woodland patches.

Climate Change Mitigation

While leaving woodlands unmanaged can appear beneficial for climate mitigation, long-term non-management may reduce woodland resilience and carbon stability.

Dense, shaded canopies with limited age and structural diversity can make woodlands more vulnerable to disease, storm damage, and climate stress.

Traditional practices like coppicing, when carried out sensitively, maintain continuous tree cover and healthy soils while supporting vigorous regrowth and sustained carbon capture.

In a fragmented UK landscape, actively managed woodlands are often better able to adapt to changing conditions, as varied structure and species composition reduce the risk of large-scale failure.

Protecting ancient woodland soils while avoiding excessive shading helps balance carbon storage with ecological resilience.

Community Engagement

Non-managed woodlands with dense shading and limited visibility can feel inaccessible or unwelcoming to many people, reducing opportunities for recreation and wellbeing benefits.

Historically, UK woodlands were working landscapes shaped by regular human use, resulting in more open, diverse environments.

Active management such as coppicing can create lighter, more varied spaces that support both wildlife and public engagement.

In today’s fragmented landscape, where accessible green spaces are limited, maintaining woodlands that feel safe and inviting is particularly important.

Involving communities in woodland management not only preserves cultural heritage but also fosters connection, education, and shared responsibility for these valued landscapes.

About this Tool

This tool allows for land managers and practitioners to explore the management options for their woodland. Select a priority aim for your woodland and find out how you can maximise the benefits of your woodland for your chosen aim. This tool is for guidance only and there are certainly many more possible aims for woodland management. The information provided here is designed to be a starting point for making decisions on habitat management that will help your woodland reach its potential.
